Utility Interconnection

Your permits get you wired up legally. Your interconnection agreement is what actually lets you tie into the grid.

South Dakota does not mandate net metering

This is the single biggest thing that catches people off guard, especially if you’ve read solar guides written for other states. South Dakota is one of only three states with no net metering law on the books. The state legislature has taken up mandatory net metering multiple times — in 2013, 2015, and 2016 — and each time declined to require it, on the reasoning that shifting those costs onto other ratepayers wasn’t in the public interest.

The South Dakota Public Utilities Commission (PUC) has been explicit about this on its own solar FAQ: South Dakota does not compensate solar generation at a net-metered, retail rate. That decision sits with the legislature and PUC, not with individual utilities, so it applies no matter who you buy power from.

What this means in practice

In a net-metering state, exporting a kWh to the grid earns you a credit worth roughly what you’d pay to buy a kWh back. In South Dakota, it doesn’t. What you’re paid instead is tied to the utility’s avoided cost — what it would have cost them to generate or buy that power elsewhere — which is typically well below the retail rate. For utilities regulated by the PUC, those buyback rates for systems under 100kW have to be filed publicly with the Commission, so you can look up your utility’s actual rate before you size a system.

Why this changes how you should size a system

In a net-metering state, oversizing your array to bank summer credits against winter usage is a reasonable strategy. In South Dakota, every kWh you export instead of use yourself is being sold at a fraction of what you’d pay to buy it back later. That’s the core reason battery storage pencils out so differently here than it does in states like Minnesota or Colorado — self-consuming your own generation, rather than exporting it, is usually worth more than what your utility will pay you for it.

Interconnection is a separate agreement from your permits

An interconnection agreement is a legally binding contract between you and your utility that sets the terms for connecting your system to their grid — how much generation is allowed, what equipment and settings are required, and what happens if something changes. It has nothing to do with your county building permit or your state wiring permit, and none of those three processes will chase down the others for you.

  1. 01

    Confirm your utility and territory

    South Dakota assigns each location an exclusive electric service territory by law (SDCL 49-34A-42) — you can’t shop utilities. Investor-owned utilities like Xcel Energy are directly regulated by the PUC; rural electric cooperatives and municipal utilities set more of their own interconnection terms, so confirm which kind you’re dealing with before assuming a process.

  2. 02

    Submit an interconnection application

    For Xcel Energy customers, this typically runs through their online Solar Application Portal, whether you or your installer submits it. Applications are usually grouped by system size — small residential rooftop systems are reviewed differently, and faster, than larger commercial ones.

  3. 03

    Pay any required study or application fees

    Larger or more complex systems can trigger an engineering review and a study fee before approval; most straightforward residential rooftop systems don’t need a full pre-application study. Fee amounts and thresholds are set by the utility’s tariffs on file with the PUC, so verify current numbers directly with your utility rather than assuming.

  4. 04

    Get your signed interconnection agreement

    Once engineering review clears, the utility issues the agreement for signature, along with any cost estimate for necessary equipment or upgrades on their side of the meter. Don’t energize your system to export before this is signed — it’s what makes the connection legal, not your local permits.

  5. 05

    Final inspection and permission to operate

    Most utilities require proof of your final electrical inspection sign-off before granting permission to operate and swapping in a meter capable of tracking two-way flow. This is the point where your permitting track and your interconnection track finally meet back up.

Can I just generate power for myself and skip interconnection?

If you’re consuming everything yourself and not selling power to anyone, you’re not required to have a wholesale agreement — the restriction is on selling power at retail outside a utility relationship, which South Dakota law reserves for licensed utilities within their assigned territory. But most grid-tied systems, including battery systems with grid backup, are still capable of backfeeding the grid under some conditions, which is exactly what an interconnection agreement is meant to govern safely. Talk to your utility before assuming an off-grid-style setup exempts you from their process.

Key numbers

  • 0 — states requiring net metering in SD (it’s one of 3 nationwide with none)
  • 2001 — year SD first adopted net metering-adjacent policy; retail net metering has never been mandated since
  • 100kW — threshold under which buyback rates must be filed publicly with the PUC
