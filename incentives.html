Incentives & Tax

South Dakota doesn’t offer a state rebate program — but two real incentives still apply.

The federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC)

The main incentive for a South Dakota homeowner is federal, not state. The residential ITC lets you credit a percentage of your total system cost — panels, batteries, inverters, and installation labor — against what you owe in federal income tax.

This is a tax credit, not a deduction — it reduces your federal tax bill dollar-for-dollar, not just your taxable income. It’s non-refundable but can typically be carried forward to future tax years if it exceeds what you owe in a given year. Confirm the current rules with a tax professional or the IRS before filing, since eligibility details (battery storage, for instance, has its own qualifying rules) can shift.

South Dakota’s property tax exemption for solar

South Dakota exempts a meaningful chunk of a solar system’s value from real property tax. Under SDCL 10-4-42 through 10-4-45, the greater of the first $50,000 of assessed value, or 70% of the assessed value, of a solar energy system under 5 megawatts is exempt from real property tax.

In plain terms: adding a solar system to your home increases what your property is worth, but South Dakota shields most or all of that added value from being reassessed and taxed the way a home addition normally would be. Check with your county director of equalization on how the exemption is actually applied to your assessment.

What South Dakota does not offer

No state income tax, no state credit

South Dakota has no state personal income tax, which also means there’s no state tax credit for solar the way you’d see in states like Colorado or New Mexico — there’s no state income tax liability to credit against. This isn’t a gap in the program; it’s a structural fact about the state, and it’s the reason the federal ITC and the property tax exemption are the two real levers here rather than three or four.

There’s also no statewide rebate or performance-payment program run by the PUC or any state agency — unlike states that pair net metering with cash-back rebates. Whatever you’re paid for exported power comes down to your specific utility’s filed buyback rate, covered on the interconnection page, not a state incentive program.

Vet who you’re buying from

The PUC’s own consumer guidance is worth repeating here: before signing with any solar company, ask for references and proof of licensure, and check the company’s standing with the South Dakota Secretary of State. Get more than one bid. A system sized and quoted without accounting for South Dakota’s lack of net metering is going to have a very different payback timeline than the salesperson’s pitch assumes.

Key numbers

  • 30% — federal ITC through 2032
  • $50,000 or 70% — whichever’s greater, exempt from SD property tax
  • $0 — SD state income tax (so no state credit exists)
