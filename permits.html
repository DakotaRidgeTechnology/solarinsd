Permits & Wiring

Two separate permits, two separate offices. Neither one is your utility.

There is no single "solar permit" in South Dakota

South Dakota has no statewide building code and no dedicated solar permitting office. In practice, a home solar install touches two separate layers of government that don't talk to each other:

  • Electrical wiring — regulated at the state level by the South Dakota Electrical Commission, part of the Department of Labor and Regulation.
  • Building / structural — regulated locally, by whatever county or city the property sits in. Requirements vary by jurisdiction.

Your utility's interconnection agreement is a third, entirely separate process — covered on the interconnection page. Don't assume getting a building permit or wiring permit means you're cleared to connect to the grid, or the reverse.

The Homeowner's Wiring Permit

South Dakota law carves out an "owner's exemption": if you own and live on the property, you can legally do your own electrical wiring — including a solar installation — without holding an electrical contractor's license. This is set out in SDCL 36-16-15 and ARSD 20:44:14:01(12).

Read the fine print

The owner's exemption only covers a residence or farmstead you personally own and live in. It does not cover rental property, a home you're actively trying to sell, or a mobile/modular home unless it sits on land you own. It also can't be used to let a licensed electrician or anyone else do the work under your permit — the exemption is personal to you.

Doing your own wiring doesn't mean doing it unpermitted. Any installation that generates an inspection fee of $30 or more needs a wiring permit before you start. For a system the size of a typical home solar install, that threshold is met immediately. The process:

  1. 01 Apply before you start work

    The Homeowner Permit Application goes to the South Dakota Electrical Commission in Pierre, by mail or fax with a check or card payment. You need an approved permit in hand before any wiring begins — not just before inspection.

    Submit at least 2 weeks ahead of your start date

  2. 02 Pay the permit and inspection fees

    There's a flat $20 permit fee plus an inspection fee calculated from the job, with a minimum inspection charge. Fee schedules are published by the Electrical Commission and are worth checking against your specific system size before you apply.

    $20 permit fee + inspection fee

  3. 03 Wire to the National Electrical Code

    Every installation, homeowner or not, has to meet the NEC and the South Dakota State Wiring Bulletin, which is updated on the same three-year cycle as the NEC. This is the same code your inspector is checking a licensed contractor's work against — the exemption changes who can legally do the labor, not what standard it's held to.

  4. 04 Call for your inspections

    You're required to contact your state electrical inspector directly to schedule inspections as the work reaches the right stages — this doesn't happen automatically once the permit's issued. For new services or grounding work, ask your inspector about the concrete-encased grounding electrode (a "Ufer ground") requirement before you close anything up.

If you're inside Sioux Falls or Rapid City

Those two cities issue their own local wiring permits instead of the state handling it directly — contact your city government, not the state Electrical Commission, if your property is within their limits. Everywhere else in the state, including unincorporated areas and most smaller cities, the state Commission is the right office.

The local building permit

Separately from wiring, most counties and cities require a building permit for roof-mounted or ground-mounted solar arrays, since you're adding structural load and, for ground mounts, a new structure on the property. What gets asked for typically includes:

  • A completed building permit application through your county or city planning/zoning office
  • A site plan showing where the array sits relative to property lines, setbacks, and easements
  • Structural details — racking attachment method, roof framing, or the foundation design for a ground mount
  • An electrical line diagram showing how the system ties into your service panel and disconnects

This is genuinely local. Lincoln County, Minnehaha County, and the cities within them each run their own process, fee schedule, and review timeline. There's no state-level shortcut here — call your county or city building/zoning department directly and ask what they need for a residential solar installation before you finalize your design.

Historic districts and HOAs

If your property is in a designated historic district, expect an additional layer of design review from the local historic preservation authority, focused on how visible the array is and how it reads against the district's character — this is on top of, not instead of, your normal building and wiring permits. If you're in a subdivision with an HOA, check your covenants too; South Dakota's permitting rules don't override a private HOA agreement.
